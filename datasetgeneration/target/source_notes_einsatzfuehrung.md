## 1. Operational Objective Framework

### Standing Mission Priority Order

1. **Sichern**

   * protect responders first
   * secure people and animals from immediate danger
   * identify and control hazards to crews and bystanders
2. **Retten**

   * remove endangered people and animals from danger zones
   * prioritize life rescue over all other operational goals
3. **Halten**

   * prevent escalation
   * contain the incident
   * stabilize the situation
4. **Schützen**

   * protect still unaffected assets
   * prevent secondary damage
5. **Bewältigen**

   * continue incident management with available resources
   * complete remaining operational tasks after stabilization

### Priority Hierarchy Across Protected Interests

1. people
2. animals
3. environment
4. property

## 2. Incident Phases Relevant for Scenario Design

### Canonical Incident Sequence

1. event occurs
2. alarm is received and processed
3. resources are dispatched
4. units respond and approach
5. on scene assessment begins
6. command decisions are made
7. operational tasks are assigned and executed
8. effects are controlled and checked
9. responsibilities are handed over
10. operational readiness is restored

### Required Phase Logic for Realistic Scenarios

* alarm information precedes deployment
* deployment precedes arrival
* arrival precedes detailed assessment
* assessment precedes major tactical decisions
* task completion must precede handover or demobilization
* incident is not complete until:

  * life safety is restored
  * further damage is not expected
  * readiness is re established
  * success has been checked

## 3. Command Process Constraints

### Mandatory Leadership Cycle

All realistic scenarios should reflect repeated use of the following cycle:

1. **Feststellen**

   * identify what is happening
   * determine where it is happening
   * gather observable facts
2. **Beurteilen**

   * assess development, hazards, means, options, and priorities
3. **Entscheiden**

   * define goal and select tactical approach
4. **Handeln**

   * issue task oriented orders and execute measures
5. **Kontrollieren**

   * verify implementation and effect of measures
   * update decisions if required

### Dataset Relevant Interpretation

* task assignments must be traceable to prior observations or reports
* updates should trigger reassessment when conditions change
* completion claims must support a control step or transition

## 4. Operational Task Families

### A. Command and Coordination

* establish command
* maintain communication links
* clarify responsibilities
* define waiting areas, access routes, and operational zones
* coordinate with dispatch and partner organizations
* request additional resources when needed
* assign sections or functional areas if complexity requires

### B. Safety and Hazard Control

* identify risks to responders and civilians
* maintain safe access and escape routes
* preserve safe operating distance
* consider collapse, electricity, explosion, hazardous substances, traffic, and weather risks
* adapt operations to time critical hazards

### C. Rescue

* locate endangered persons or animals
* prioritize rescue before property protection
* assess number and location of endangered persons
* keep escape and attack routes usable

### D. Fire Containment and Suppression

* prevent escalation
* contain extension to adjacent spaces or structures
* attack fire from tactically sound access points
* use building compartments and closed doors as opportunities for containment
* monitor smoke, heat, and structural behavior continuously

### E. Ventilation and Smoke Control

* assess smoke spread spatially and vertically
* use natural or supported smoke extraction if tactically justified
* opening doors and windows must be treated as tactically consequential
* smoke control should support rescue, interior attack, and damage limitation

### F. Traffic, Access, and Scene Organization

* secure access routes
* manage road closures and diversions if needed
* define staging, equipment depot, and operational command location
* organize incoming and outgoing vehicle movement

### G. Logistics and Resource Management

* check personnel and material availability
* account for water supply, refill time, and transport constraints
* request additional means when existing means are insufficient or delayed
* maintain or restore readiness

### H. Handover and Demobilization

* release unnecessary resources once justified
* transfer responsibility to the appropriate authority or organization
* complete checks before final handover
* restore operational readiness materially and personally

## 5. Role Specific Responsibilities

### Einsatzleiter

* carries total responsibility throughout the incident
* sets priorities according to the standing mission
* determines tactical order of operations
* issues clear, feasible, short, task oriented orders
* checks whether orders are understood
* assigns sections or functional responsibilities if scale or complexity requires
* defines own location when not obvious
* keeps command continuity through delegation and deputy arrangements
* integrates partner organizations and interfaces

### Abschnittsoffizier or Bereichsverantwortlicher

* receives a framework task or responsibility area
* acts independently within the intent of command
* coordinates assigned resources
* requests further means from command when needed
* reports progress, obstacles, and completion evidence

### Specialized Functional Leads

Possible areas include:

* medical
* hygiene
* care
* environment
* information
* logistics
* safety

Constraints:

* role outputs must match the role's domain
* role authority is bounded by assigned area and operational intent
* requests for more resources must flow upward to command

### Dispatch or Early Coordination Function

* processes initial alarm
* collects core information:

  * what
  * where
  * whether people or animals are endangered
  * how
  * how many
  * additional clarifications
* transmits the most important information in structured form
* exchanges updates with field units on dispatch, arrival, short situation report, and incident end

## 6. Order Design Constraints

### Core Requirements for Valid Orders

* explicit
* task oriented
* feasible
* short

### Required Order Content Structure

Orders should map to:

* **What** must be done
* **Where** it must be done
* **With what** it should be done

### Optional Additions When Needed

* short orientation to the situation
* command intent
* special hazards or special instructions
* command location if not obvious

### Readback Rule

* the receiver repeats the assignment
* command remains responsible for ensuring correct understanding

### Assignment Granularity Constraint

* one recipient should receive one task at a time
* multiple simultaneous tasks to the same recipient increase ambiguity and should be avoided unless explicitly structured

## 7. Typical Task Sequences

### Generic Incident Sequence

1. receive structured alarm
2. dispatch and confirm turnout
3. approach while preparing mentally and tactically
4. gather observations on access, hazards, and development
5. decide immediate priorities
6. assign first operational tasks
7. receive progress reports and completion signals
8. adapt tactics and request further means if required
9. stabilize situation
10. perform control checks
11. hand over responsibility
12. restore readiness

### Typical Building Fire Sequence

1. identify access and attack route
2. assess rescue needs
3. secure responders and key hazards
4. secure stairwell or escape route
5. perform interior attack or equivalent suppression measure
6. contain smoke and fire spread
7. protect unaffected side or compartments
8. ventilate when tactically appropriate
9. verify extinguishment and residual safety
10. hand over scene after control measures and checks

### Traffic or External Safety Support Sequence

1. receive area responsibility
2. define closure or diversion boundary
3. establish control measures
4. report traffic condition or side effects
5. lift restrictions when command authorizes

## 8. Dependencies Between Tasks

### Safety Dependencies

* no interior task should ignore responder safety constraints
* unsafe structure, electricity, explosion risk, or hazardous substance presence can restrict task feasibility
* safe arrival and safe positioning precede tactical action

### Rescue Dependencies

* rescue priority depends on credible indication of endangered persons or animals
* choice of rescue route depends on access and smoke or fire conditions
* preserved escape and attack routes are enabling conditions

### Fire Suppression Dependencies

* suppression depends on access, water, and feasible attack path
* fire spread assessment depends on smoke and building structure observations
* compartment closure can be a precondition for controlling spread

### Ventilation Dependencies

* ventilation should be linked to smoke conditions and tactical objective
* opening structures without assessment may worsen fire or smoke spread
* ventilation actions should be consistent with reported fire location and path

### Handover Dependencies

* handover requires evidence that acute danger is over
* final release requires successful control checks
* demobilization requires restoration of operational readiness

## 9. Building Fire Specific Realism Constraints

### Required Considerations

* smoke and heat development must be observed continuously
* structural behavior and collapse risk must be reassessed during the incident
* stairwells are both escape routes and fire service attack routes
* brandabschnitte and closed fire doors can function as containment opportunities
* visible smoke characteristics can inform assessment of internal development
* object knowledge and plans can be used as tactical aids

### Plausible Tactical Concerns

* access to the building and to the fire compartment
* vertical and horizontal spread
* open versus closed doors and windows
* vulnerable structural elements
* roof space and concealed voids
* need for smoke exhaust
* water supply sufficiency and refill constraints

### Valid Tactical Actions

* securing stairwell
* limiting spread through compartment closure
* extinguishing at identified seat of fire
* protecting unaffected areas
* conducting ventilation aligned with conditions
* verifying final safety through checks and measurements where applicable

## 10. Valid Completion Evidence

### Completion Evidence Must Be Observable, Measurable, or Operationally Verifiable

### Examples of Valid Completion Evidence

* endangered persons removed from danger zone
* no further persons credibly reported in the hazard area
* fire extinguished at identified seat
* smoke spread reduced after closure or ventilation measures
* road closure or diversion physically established
* additional resource request fulfilled and deployed
* final control checks indicate no remaining acute hazard
* scene formally handed over to the responsible authority
* units report restored readiness or return to service

### Invalid or Weak Completion Evidence

* unsupported claim that situation is safe without any observation or check
* task declared complete without indicating effect
* handover before stabilization or final control
* role reporting completion for a task outside its assigned authority or visibility

## 11. Realistic Scenario Design Constraints

### Realism Requirements

* scenarios must reflect dynamic, uncertain, and time pressured operations
* not all facts need to be known at the start
* command decisions may occur with incomplete information
* updates may change assessment and trigger new decisions
* resource limits and access constraints should be plausible
* partner organizations may be present when incident scale justifies it

### Boundaries on Complexity

* complexity should arise from realistic uncertainty, dependencies, and evolving information
* arbitrary randomness without tactical consequence is not realistic
* scenario developments must correspond to physically plausible evolution over time

### Role Consistency Constraints

* each actor may only observe, decide, or report what is plausible from role and position
* command can assign and reprioritize
* subareas can report local observations and request support
* dispatch can update alarm and resource status
* partner organizations should speak within their domain

---

## 12. Traceable Constraints for Dataset Generation

### Constraints Derived from Operational Doctrine

* every scenario should encode the standing mission priorities
* task ordering must be justifiable through safety, rescue, containment, protection, and resolution logic
* orders should be short, feasible, and tied to what, where, and with what
* command must verify understanding through readback or equivalent confirmation
* completion must be evidenced, not merely asserted
* incident closure requires:

  * restored life safety
  * no expected follow on damage
  * handover where relevant
  * readiness restoration
  * success control
* building fire scenarios must include realistic consideration of smoke spread, access, and structural risk
* delegation into responsibility areas is valid only when scale or complexity justifies it
